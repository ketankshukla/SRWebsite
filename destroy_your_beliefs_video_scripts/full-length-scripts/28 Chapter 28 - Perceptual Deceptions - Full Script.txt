Perceptual Deceptions
Seeing Beyond Illusions

Welcome back. Today we're examining the very foundation of knowledge: perception.

You trust your senses. You see the world as it is. Right?

Not exactly. Your brain doesn't show you reality. It shows you a construction—a best guess based on incomplete information and prior expectations.

Your eyes have a blind spot where the optic nerve connects. You should see a gap in your vision. But you don't—your brain fills it in.

When you watch a movie, you see smooth motion. But it's actually still images flashing rapidly. Your brain constructs the experience of movement.

Optical illusions aren't glitches—they're windows into how perception normally works. Your brain is always interpreting, not just receiving.

Emotions shape perception. When you're afraid, neutral faces look threatening. When you're happy, the world looks brighter.

Attention is selective. You miss obvious things when you're focused elsewhere.

So what does this mean?

Your experience of reality is not reality itself. It's a model your brain builds—useful, but not complete or perfectly accurate.

This should make you humble. What seems obviously true based on direct experience might be a construction.

It should make you curious. What else might you be missing?

It should make you open. When someone describes an experience differently, they might be perceiving differently.

Your senses are tools, not windows. They help you navigate the world, but they don't show you the world as it truly is.
