The free market will solve everything. Just get government out of the way and let competition work its magic.

You've heard this before. It's one of the most powerful beliefs of our time. But is it true?

Let's look at reality.

First, there's no such thing as a truly "free" market. Every market operates within rules—laws, regulations, property rights, contract enforcement. Even the most capitalist economies have governments setting the rules of the game.

Second, the world's most successful economies aren't pure free markets. The United States, Germany, Japan, South Korea—they all used significant government intervention to build their prosperity. Public education, infrastructure, research funding, industrial policy. The "free market vs. government" debate is a false choice.

Third, markets fail. A lot.

The 2008 financial crisis happened because under-regulated markets created massive risks that crashed the global economy. Climate change is happening because markets don't account for environmental costs—economists call these "externalities."

Markets are great at some things: coordinating complex activity, driving innovation, responding to consumer preferences. But they're terrible at others: providing public goods, preventing monopolies, thinking long-term, distributing resources fairly.

Here's what often gets ignored: many of our greatest innovations came from public investment, not private markets. The internet, GPS, touch screens, the basic research behind most pharmaceuticals—all funded by governments.

And about that "invisible hand" that supposedly guides markets to good outcomes? Adam Smith, who coined the term, also wrote extensively about the need for moral considerations in economics and the limitations of markets. His ideas have been oversimplified.

So what's the alternative?

It's not abandoning markets. It's seeing them clearly—as tools, not gods.

Markets work well when properly regulated and complemented by strong public institutions. They need rules to prevent monopolies, protect workers, account for environmental costs, and ensure fair competition.

The question isn't "free market or government control." It's "what mix of tools best serves human flourishing?"

That might mean stronger antitrust enforcement. Universal healthcare. Public investment in research and infrastructure. Carbon pricing. Worker protections.

When you stop treating the free market as sacred truth, you can start thinking creatively about what actually works.

Economics isn't physics. There are no immutable laws. There are just choices about how we organize society—and we can make better ones.
